## Post 2 — Day 6 — Works Cafe Westford build story

**Hook:** *Richard French gave us a build date. We worked backward from it.*

In QSR, target-open isn't a marketing milestone. It's a P&L number.

Every day past target-open is rent, payroll, lost royalties, and a marketing campaign with nothing to point at.

When Richard French — the founder and CEO of Works Cafe — handed us the Westford build, he didn't ask "when can you start?" He asked "what do you need to hit the day I've already announced?"

That's a different conversation. Most GCs work forward from groundbreaking. Owners work backward from opening day.

So we worked backward.

We sequenced long-lead equipment before we sequenced trades. We held a brand-spec review on day one, not at framing inspection. We kept Richard in the field every two weeks — not because he had to be, but because the kind of operator who'd ask that question is the kind of operator who wants to see it.

Opened on the day we said.

The full case study is on the new site. If you've got a build with a real opening date on it, this is the read.

## Post 3 — Day 11 — Numbers post on cost-of-cheap-GC

**Hook:** *The 8% you save on a cheap bid usually costs you 22%.*

Math from the last three years of jobs we got called into after the original GC walked or stalled:

→ Average bid delta on the original "cheap" award: 8% under our number.
→ Average change-order overrun by completion: +14% over the cheap bid.
→ Average delay past target-open: 31 days.
→ Lost royalties + rent during delay: typically another 6-9% of total project cost.

Net result: the franchisee paid 22-28% more than our original number, opened a month late, and spent the first quarter trying to recover the launch.

This isn't a sales argument. It's the actual data from the rescue jobs in our file cabinet.

If your next build is on a brand with a tight spec book and a hard target-open, the lowest bid isn't the cheapest number. It's the most expensive one written down differently.

We built a piece on the new site that walks through this in detail — *What franchisees lose when they pick the lowest bid*. Aimed at exactly one reader: the operator weighing two quotes right now.

## Post 5 — Day 22 — "We manage your money like it's ours"

**Hook:** *My dad has said the same sentence to every client for thirty years.*

*"We manage your money like it's ours."*

I used to think that was just a thing contractors say. Then I started running jobs and figured out it's actually a policy.

Here's what it means in practice:

→ We don't pad the contingency for our comfort.
→ We don't sole-source trades when a competitive bid would save the owner six points.
→ We don't slow-walk a change order to bury a margin recovery.
→ We tell you when a brand-spec line item is over-engineered and where you can value-engineer without breaking compliance.

The cheaper GCs in our market do the opposite of every one of those bullets. That's how their bid is lower in week one and higher in month six.

We've turned down jobs where the owner wanted us to behave like the cheaper GC. We're not going to start now.

That's the part of the business my dad built that I'd never trade for growth.
