What franchisees lose when they pick the lowest bid.

A QSR construction reality check from a builder who’s been on both sides of the bid.

Mark Geissler, CEO · Repro Construction May 15, 2026 8 min read
In this piece
  1. The change-order problem
  2. The schedule problem
  3. The brand-spec problem
  4. The accountability problem
  5. The math, side by side

Most of the franchisees who end up calling us called somebody cheaper first. They liked our number. They liked our references. Then they took the bid that was eight percent lower and figured the savings would carry the day. Six months later we’re on the phone again, usually about a store that opened late, audited badly, or both. This is the math we walk them through next time.

I’ve been building restaurants in New England for thirty years — over 200 projects across Dunkin’, Jersey Mike’s, The Works Cafe, Wayback Burger, Inspire Brands, Yella, and Zo. The bid spread between us and the next GC is rarely the whole story, and on a QSR build it’s almost never the right story. There are four places the gap closes — and usually inverts — once construction actually starts. Worth knowing before you sign anything.

One more thing up front. The point of this piece isn’t to talk anyone out of the cheap bid. Sometimes the cheap bid is the right call — if the GC has built your brand a dozen times and is running lean on a fast-track they’ll repeat next quarter, take it. The point is to make sure the bid number you compared isn’t a different math problem from the bid number you signed. Most of the time, it is.

01 · The bid that grows The change-order problem.

When a GC bids low, they have to recover margin somewhere. The honest ones recover it through productivity. The rest recover it through change orders, and that’s where the lowest bid stops being the lowest bid.

Construction industry rules of thumb put change orders at roughly 5 to 15 percent of contract value on commercial work. That range comes from decades of CMAA and AIA data and it’s the number you should expect on a clean job with a competent GC. On a brand-spec QSR build the upper bound climbs, because the spec book is unforgiving and any gap between “what the drawings call for” and “what the GC actually built” turns into a change order. On QSR work with a GC who’s learning the brand on your store, 15 to 20 percent is normal, and we’ve seen it land higher.

Run the numbers. Say the cheap bid is $850,000 and ours is $920,000 — an eight-percent spread, the kind of gap that talks a franchisee out of our number. Change orders on the cheap bid at 18 percent add $153,000. The cheap bid’s true landing cost is $1,003,000. Ours, with the spec priced in up front and change orders kept inside 5 percent: $966,000. The cheaper bid landed $37,000 higher than the “more expensive” one, and that’s before the store was a day late.

The trick is that the cheap GC’s change orders don’t arrive as one bill. They arrive across the project, framed as “the drawings weren’t clear” or “the brand rep changed the spec.” By the time you add them up, the budget conversation feels like a series of small concessions instead of the original bid being wrong. It was wrong. You just paid for it the slow way.

An honest builder prices the spec into the bid. A low builder prices the spec into the change order. The franchisee pays either way — the only question is whether they knew the number going in.

What this looks like in practice: the variance log. Before mobilization, we sit down with the brand drawings, the leased shell’s as-built conditions, and the prototype book, and we produce a written variance log — every place the spec and the shell don’t line up, every place the drawings could be interpreted two ways, every place a substitution might be needed. Owner signs off. That document becomes the boundary line between scope and change order. If something comes up that wasn’t in the log, it’s a real change. If something comes up that was, it’s already priced. Most of the “the drawings weren’t clear” change orders that hit a franchisee on the back half of a job are change orders that should have been resolved on paper before anyone framed a wall.

02 · The calendar that costs The schedule problem.

Every closed day in a leased restaurant shell is the single most expensive line item a franchisee never sees on a bid sheet. There’s no row on the proposal that reads “two-week schedule slippage: $74,000.” But that’s the bill, and it lands on the operator, not the GC.

Use Dunkin’ as the example. Average Dunkin’ AUV ran in the neighborhood of $1.3 million in recent franchise disclosure documents — roughly $3,500 a day in store revenue at the average. Two weeks closed past target-open is about $49,000 of lost top-line. Add rent on a leased shell — commonly $5,000 to $10,000 over a two-week stretch in eastern Massachusetts. Add pre-opening payroll on staff you’ve already hired, trained, and scheduled. Realistic all-in cost of a two-week overrun on a Dunkin’ opening: $60,000 to $80,000. Frequently more than the original bid gap that made the cheaper GC look attractive.

Jersey Mike’s runs a similar profile — system AUV around $1.1 million, daily revenue near $3,000, same rent and payroll drag. A two-week overrun on a Mike’s store lands in the $50,000 to $70,000 range. Higher-volume concepts go higher; the math doesn’t change, only the multiplier.

And royalties. A franchisee pays royalties on system revenue, but they only earn revenue on open days. Slippage costs the franchisee the revenue and costs the brand the royalty — which is why brand development directors care about schedule certainty too, and why a GC’s on-time record matters to your relationship with the brand, not just your P&L.

We price the calendar like it’s a line item. Target-open date gets handed to the staffing manager, the marketing team, and the landlord. We work backward from it. The cheap bid doesn’t price the calendar because the cheap GC isn’t the one paying for the slippage.

The math gets sharper at the top of the market. A Chipotle averages roughly $3.2 million in AUV across the system — about $8,800 a day. A two-week overrun on a Chipotle build costs the operator more than $120,000 in lost revenue alone, before rent and payroll. If you’re a multi-unit operator running five or ten stores, a single schedule miss can fund the entire next franchise fee. This is why brand development directors at the bigger systems are picky about which GCs land on their approved lists: schedule risk on construction is brand risk on launch.

03 · The audit you fail The brand-spec compliance problem.

Inspire Brands, Dunkin’, The Works Cafe, Wayback Burger, Jersey Mike’s — every one of these brands has a prototype book. The book specifies finishes, fixtures, counter geometry, signage placement, lighting temperature, hood configuration, brand-compliant millwork, the works. The book is not a suggestion. The brand audit measures against it.

A GC who’s built your brand before reads the book before they bid. They know what the bake case is supposed to look like. They know which countertop laminate is on-spec and which one looks close enough on a sample but flags on inspection. They know where the brand rep is going to put their finger when they walk the store.

A GC who hasn’t built your brand reads the book after they bid. Sometimes after they’ve started framing. Then the brand rep shows up at first inspection, fingers go on the wrong things, and the rework starts. Brand-flag rejections on QSR builds typically cost the franchisee three ways:

  • Direct rework. Tear out the non-compliant element, re-source the compliant one, re-install. On a bake case or a counter, that’s easily $15,000 to $40,000 of materials and labor.
  • Re-inspection fees and brand-rep escalation. Brand reps don’t fly back free. Some brands charge re-inspection fees directly. All of them remember the operator whose first store didn’t pass.
  • Delayed grand opening. Compound interest on the schedule problem above. A failed brand audit doesn’t just cost rework dollars — it pushes the open and re-bills every day of slippage at the rates we already walked through.

Experienced QSR builders treat the prototype book as the floor, not the upsell. It’s the baseline cost of being in this business. If your GC asks for a copy of the spec book after the bid is awarded, you’re going to pay for that education twice — once in the bid, once in the rework.

And brand reps remember. The first store a franchisee opens sets the relationship with the brand’s development team for every store after. A clean opening puts you on the list for future territory. A messy one puts you on the list of operators who get assigned the careful-watching treatment for the next three sites. That isn’t bid-sheet math, but it’s real, and it’s the kind of cost a franchisee only sees in retrospect.

04 · The phone that doesn’t ring The accountability problem.

The fourth gap is the hardest to price and the one franchisees complain about most. The cheap GC hands you off to a project coordinator who answers email twice a week and routes the hard calls to someone else. The harder the decision, the longer the response time. Three days to get a callback on a punch-list item that’s holding the certificate of occupancy is not unusual.

This is the “owner-side accountability” argument made specific. On a Repro job, my name is on the company and I answer the phone. Nick — my project lead — is on jobsites the way a multi-unit operator is on theirs. Chris Sibulkin is the superintendent on site, daily, through demo and rough-in and finish. When something needs a decision, the person who can actually make the decision is the one who picks up.

The dollar cost of accountability is harder to model than change orders or schedule, but it shows up in the same places. A coordinator who can’t green-light a substitution waits for an owner who’s in another meeting. The job slows. The schedule slips. The franchisee pays. A coordinator who doesn’t know the spec authorizes the wrong substitution. The brand audit fails. The franchisee pays.

We say it the way Mark’s said it for thirty years: we manage your money like it’s ours. That phrase only means something if the owner’s the one on the call. Otherwise it’s a brochure line. On our jobs, it’s the operating model.

If you want to test it on a GC you’re considering, ask one question: who do I call on Saturday at 7pm if the inspector failed the rough-in and Monday’s pour is in question? The honest answer tells you whether you’re hiring an owner or hiring a coordinator. Both are fine answers. They are not the same price.

None of the four arguments above are theoretical. They’re what we’ve seen in 200 restaurant projects across New England, and they’re the reason franchisees who hire us once tend to hire us again. They’re also why the prospects who pick the cheap GC sometimes call back. The change orders, the schedule slip, the brand-flag rework, the coordinator who didn’t pick up — all of it shows up in the same six months, and by then the savings on the original bid are gone.

The way to avoid being that franchisee is to run the math before you sign, not after. That’s the next section.

FAQ

Questions we get from franchisees comparing bids.

What’s a typical change-order percentage on QSR construction?

Industry rules of thumb put change orders at roughly 5 to 15 percent of contract value on commercial construction. On brand-spec QSR work — where the GC has to hit a prototype book they haven’t built before — the upper bound climbs. A GC learning a brand’s spec on your store will routinely push the change-order line into the 15 to 20 percent range, and we’ve seen cases where it lands higher. Experienced QSR builders price the spec into the original bid; learning GCs price it back in later.

How much does a two-week schedule overrun cost a Dunkin’ franchisee?

Average Dunkin’ AUV ran around $1.3 million in recent franchise disclosure documents — roughly $3,500 in daily store revenue. On a two-week overrun (14 calendar days), the franchisee loses about $49,000 of top-line revenue, plus rent on a closed shell (commonly $5,000 to $10,000 for the period), plus pre-opening payroll on staff already hired. Realistic all-in cost of a two-week delay on a Dunkin’ opening lands in the $60,000 to $80,000 range — frequently more than the original bid gap that made the cheaper GC look attractive.

Why do cheap bids end up more expensive?

Three reasons stack. First, a GC who bids low has to recover margin somewhere, and change orders are where that recovery lives. Second, a GC who hasn’t built your brand before learns the spec on your job, which produces brand-flag rejections at inspection and rework on the back end. Third, schedule slippage is the most expensive line item a franchisee never sees on the bid sheet — every closed day in a leased shell is rent, payroll, and lost royalties hitting at once. Sum those three and the low bid is usually the high final number.
